**Status:** fetch. **URL:** https://www.lexon.org/docs/Merging%20traditional%20contracts%20(or%20law)%20and%20(smart)%20e-contracts%20%E2%80%93%20a%20novel%20approach.pdf
**Axes:** A1, A5. **Output:** `out/L05_convergence.md`

Idelberger's provocation, from the abstract: the machine does not have to understand anything to be useful, as long as it translates comprehensively while keeping syntax and semantics intact. This is the anti-comprehension thesis, and it cuts across both Lexon's and agentprivacy's claims. Handle it as the adversarial letter of the set.

## Read for

1. **The anti-comprehension thesis versus performative agreement (A1).** If translation without understanding suffices, what work does the ceremony do? Candidate answer to test: the ceremony does not require the machine to understand either; it requires the parties to demonstrate understanding (the proverb derivation is proof-of-understanding, per the Understanding as Key paradigm). Idelberger relocates understanding away from the machine; Understanding as Key relocates it to the human boundary. These may be the same move viewed from different layers. Confirm or break this.
2. **Integrated contract stack (A5).** Idelberger surveys competitors for computable contracts (DSLs, natural language approaches, logic languages) with an eye to merging traditional and e-contracts into one stack. Extract his stack model and place the agentprivacy layers on it: MyTerms terms at the top, agreement ceremony as formation, VRC as record, micro-blockchain-style logs or chain as evidence, gates/Solidity as enforcement. Identify which layer his survey has no candidate for. Expected gap: the bilateral relationship memory layer. If confirmed, that gap is the headline finding of this chronicle.
3. **Legal looks.** His evaluation criterion of "legal looks" (does the artefact read as law to lawyers) applies directly to MyTerms adoption: a term-set that does not look legal will not be honoured by counsel. Note his assessment of which languages pass.
