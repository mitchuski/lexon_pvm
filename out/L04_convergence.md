## What the letter claims (5 sentences maximum, no quotes over 14 words)

Smart legal contracts turn on a single question: is the code faithful to the parties' agreement, where validation, building the right code, is far harder than verification, building the code right. Fidelity failures arise all down a language stack running from natural language through specification, programming, assembly, and executable layers, compounded by a semantic and pragmatic gap between lawyers and programmers. Semantic specification of an agreement must cover deontic, operational, and temporal aspects, and faces three named obstacles: the separability, isomorphism, and canonical form problems. Computable Contracts, a specific methodology within the broader Smart Contracts field, uses a single artefact, a controlled natural language that is also a domain-specific language, as both contract and code, collapsing a five-step validation task to three steps. Clack names Lexon and Logical English as the closest current CNL/DSL candidates, with Lexon expressing actions and Logical English expressing deontic obligations, neither yet evaluated on large complex contracts.

## Convergence findings

### A1: Performative agreement

Clack's core question, stated in the Abstract and Introduction (pp.1-2), is confidence that code is faithful to the intentions of the parties. His verification/validation distinction (Section 1, p.3) hands the run a precise vocabulary: verification builds the code right, validation builds the right code. On that split, Lexon's readability and the RPP's bilaterality are both validation mechanisms, not verification mechanisms.

A four-stage fidelity-failure taxonomy can be assembled from the letter. Stage 1, drafting: ambiguity and vagueness in the text, plus implied terms and applicable law living in linguistic pragmatics outside the text (Section 2.1 p.10, Section 3.2 p.18). Stage 2, translation: error-prone manual conversion from uncontrolled natural language, lawyer/programmer terms-of-art collisions (execution, performance, interpretation, construction), and the ante hoc versus post hoc semantics mismatch (Section 2.1 pp.9-10, Section 3 pp.14-15). Stage 3, down-stack execution: compilers, optimisers, and runtime systems can introduce semantic distortion, including documented compiler bugs and data races (Section 3.3 pp.20-21, footnote 68). Stage 4, interpretation: post hoc legal construction may diverge from the ante hoc semantics encoded in code (footnote 50, p.15).

Placement on that taxonomy: Lexon's readability attacks stage 2 by merging the top two stack layers (Section 4.3 p.27), realising Clack's Strategy 4, where the contract is the source code (Section 2.3 pp.12-13), and it collapses the five-step validation task (Section 4 p.22) to three steps (pp.22-23). Bilaterality attacks stage 1 and the pre-textual layer: Allen's contract stack layer (i), the spoken words of negotiation (Section 1 pp.3-4), plus the residue Clack flags even for Strategy 4, namely that the source code is not the entire expression of the agreement (p.13). The brief's conjecture that readability and bilaterality address different failure stages and compose rather than compete is supported by this placement. It remains a conjecture, testable in the Diedrich session.

Clack also identifies a residual gap Lexon cannot close alone: even with the single artefact, step 2 of the reduced task remains, validating the artefact against the agreement as a whole (Section 4 p.23). Bilateral co-derivation is a candidate mechanism for exactly that residual step. Conjecture.

Two further placements. First, Clack's internal/external model distinction (Section 2.2 p.11, drawing on ISDA/Linklaters and the UKJT statement that contractual obligations may be defined by computer code) gives neutral, court-adjacent vocabulary for the performative claim: Lexon contracts and VRCs both choose the internal model, where the artefact constitutes the obligation rather than implementing it. Second, on Computable Contracts as methodology: Clack distinguishes Smart Contracts, platform-agnostic automation since Szabo (Introduction p.2), from Computable Contracts, a single artefact serving as contract plus code plus optionally semantic specification (Section 4 pp.22-23, term from Surden, footnote 73). MyTerms agreements sit cleanly in his framework as computable before they are smart: machine-readable and deterministic, no chain required. Clack cites Goodenough's point that computable form helps before any encoding into software (Section 4.3 p.28), which directly supports the contractual privacy paradigm framing.

### A5: Semantics of natural versus computer languages

Position (a), Clack's map: the five-layer language stack (natural, specification, programming, assembly, executable; Section 1 pp.4-8) crossed with a spectrum of approaches in Section 4: markup and templates (4.1, pp.23-24), domain-specific programming languages (4.2, pp.25-27), and CNL/DSL hybrids (4.3, pp.27-30). He places Lexon himself in 4.3 (p.29): a combined CNL and DSL targeting generation of smart contract code, with extensible grammar, a formal syntax definition still in development, a small core operational semantics, and Solidity, Javascript, and Sophia output.

The keystone-relevant contrast is Figure 2 versus Figure 3 (p.29): Logical English expresses deontic aspects, obligations; Lexon expresses actions, the operational aspect. Clack characterises Lexon as "automation of performance rather than analysis of contract semantics" (Section 4.3, p.29). Combined with footnotes 44 and 102, where deontics operate at a meta-level over actions, and the Section 4.2 caveat (pp.26-27) that no DSL has yet demonstrated deontic subtlety sufficient for high-value drafting, the picture is exact: Lexon's subject-predicate-object triples are operational edges, and the deontic layer is precisely where the promise-graph overlay, Promise and Commitment primitives as typed edges, would sit. Clack independently identifies the gap the run proposes to fill.

The slot question for position (c), the RPP's meaning-in-relationship: Clack's framework has no slot for meaning deliberately vested outside any document in a bilateral decoder. His nearest neighbours are all hazard categories, not design goals: unwritten understanding between writer and reader, and applicable law, are pragmatics to be managed (Section 2.1 p.10); deliberately vague clauses are tolerated only as the price of reaching agreement at all (Section 3.2 p.18); human-discretion points are listed as an open research challenge (Section 4.3 p.30). His conclusion (p.32, via Smith and Grice) even proposes formalising implicature into the artefact, the exact opposite move. Verdict: position (c) falls outside his taxonomy. The brief said either answer is a finding; this is the answer.

His three semantic-specification problems (Section 3.2 pp.18-19) transfer directly to the grammar-to-graph keystone. Separability, deontic, operational, and temporal aspects intertwined, means graph edges need three aspect types. Isomorphism, clause-to-specification mapping being many-to-many, means clause-to-subgraph mapping is many-to-many. Canonical form, no standard structure making same-meaning contracts structurally identical, means Lexon-emitted subgraphs from semantically equal contracts may differ, so graph deduplication and equivalence queries need a canonicalisation pass.

Two supporting observations. The Church-Rosser point (Section 3.3 p.20), that declarative languages are order-independent while imperative code must be read in written order, supports the graph-serialisation claim only for the declarative fraction of Lexon; clause sequencing with state, such as the terminate step in the Figure 3 netting example, is order-bearing and must be represented as temporal edges, not bare triples. Conjecture. And Clack's ambiguity versus vagueness taxonomy (Section 3.2 p.18: ambiguity is a small set of candidate meanings, vagueness is indeterminate or deferred meaning) fits the bilateral proverb in neither category: precise inside the dyad, indeterminate outside it. Clack classifies meaning by text-intrinsic properties; relationship-indexed meaning is a missing dimension. This is the reconstruction ceiling R < 1 stated linguistically.

Finally, an evaluation rubric of 10 criteria can be assembled from the letter for the synthesis: (1) readily generated, understood, and used by lawyers (Conclusion pp.31-32); (2) translatable directly down the language stack (p.32); (3) flexibility and expressive capability for the drafter's intended obligations (Section 2.2 p.11); (4) clean interfacing between natural-language and formal portions (p.11); (5) representation of known and not-fully-known meaning, including vague words and deliberately vague clauses (Section 4.3 p.30); (6) representation of human-discretion points (p.30); (7) complex temporal aspects (p.30); (8) modularity, understanding a clause without reading the whole contract (pp.30-31); (9) deontic subtlety (pp.26-27); (10) standardisation benefits (p.28).

## Divergences and false friends

- No speech acts anywhere. Clack never invokes Austin, performativity, or felicity conditions. His fidelity question is descriptive (does code match intent), not performative (does utterance constitute the act). The A1 performative framing falls outside his taxonomy; the closest available slot is the internal model. This absence is itself a finding, not a defect.
- False friend documented by Clack himself: execution means signing to a lawyer and running code to a programmer (Section 3 p.14). The run's own vocabulary, in which an agreement ceremony executes, should be audited against his table of colliding terms of art.
- Actor divergence: Clack's validation is expert-driven and effectively trilateral, requiring lawyers, programmers, and a validator with full dual-domain understanding (Section 1 p.3, Section 3 pp.15-16). The RPP ceremony is bilateral and party-driven with no third expert. Clack's framework has no slot for two non-expert parties validating by symmetric derivation.
- Tempering the keystone: Clack records that Lexon aims at automation of performance rather than semantic analysis, and that its formal syntax definition is still in development (p.29). The claim that a Lexon text is a lossless graph serialisation with computable meaning therefore rests on a grammar whose formal semantics Clack rates as in-progress, and on an operational, not deontic, fragment. The keystone stands as conjectured, not proven, at L04.
- False friend: meaning outside the document. For Clack it is a hazard (implied terms, applicable law, pragmatics threatening validation, Section 2.1 p.10); for the RPP it is the design asset (the bilateral decoder). Same phrase, opposite valence; the synthesis must not conflate them.
- False friend at field level: smart contract itself, Ethereum stored procedures versus Szabo's platform-agnostic automation, 17 years apart (Introduction p.2). Clack's portmanteau definition (p.2) is the neutral bridge term.
- Layer warning: even a perfect top-of-stack grammar-to-graph mapping says nothing about execution fidelity below it (Section 3.3 pp.20-21). The triple store is not the execution; the graph keystone is a claim about one layer of a stack, and the letter shows every layer can betray it.

## Extractable mechanisms (what ports into agentprivacy V5/V6, concretely)

1. Adopt verify/validate as V5/V6 pipeline vocabulary: verify a VRC (well-formed, signatures check) versus validate a VRC (faithful to the relationship intent). Bilaterality is then formally a validation mechanism. Feeds HENNING_BRIEF probe (i).
2. Use the five-step validation task (Section 4 p.22) as an audit checklist for any MyTerms execution layer; the 5-to-3 collapse quantifies exactly what a Lexon-serialised MyTerms agreement buys. Candidate PVM queue entry: CTR-LEX on validation-step reduction as measurable value.
3. Classify agentprivacy artefacts on the internal/external axis: the VRC is internal-model (the record defines the obligation); MyTerms term references are external-model pointers. Court-legible via the UKJT citation.
4. Canonicalisation pass for the Lexon-to-graph compiler target (P3 artefact iii): define a canonical AST form so semantically equal contracts emit identical subgraphs. Without it the canonical form problem (p.19) breaks graph deduplication and same-meaning queries. Candidate CTR-LEX seed.
5. Three-aspect edge typing for the promise-graph overlay: deontic, operational, and temporal (Section 3.2 p.18) as first-class edge classes, with promise and commitment edges as deontic meta-edges pointing at operational action edges, matching footnote 44's meta-level structure. Concrete input to BONFIRES_NOTE and HENNING_BRIEF probe (iii).
6. Pitch line for the Diedrich session: promise-typed edges supply the deontic meta-layer Clack says action-oriented CNL/DSLs have not demonstrated (pp.26-27, Figure 2 versus Figure 3), without changing the Lexon grammar: an overlay, not a language fork.
7. Extend Clack's two-way meaning taxonomy to a triad for V6 prose: ambiguous, vague, relationship-indexed. The third category is the proverb, and R < 1 is its formal statement. Candidate PVM queue framing for the A5 reconstruction-ceiling entry.
8. Reuse the 10-criterion rubric in the synthesis to score Lexon and a hypothetical RPP/MyTerms DSL side by side, as the brief's watch-for requests.
9. Positioning sentence for the paradigm, citable to the Surden-Clack lineage: MyTerms is computable contracting for the personal data relationship, computable before smart.
10. Clack's rapid-prototyping fallback (Section 3 p.16, repeated specify/platform/build cycles at increasing complexity) maps onto progressive trust escalation across repeated ceremonies. Conjecture, flagged for the synthesis.

## Open questions

1. Does the agreement ceremony precede Clack's step 1 (establishing the agreement's ante hoc meaning) or replace it? If it precedes, readability and bilaterality compose exactly; test with Diedrich.
2. In internal-model terms, are VRC parties bound by what the record does or what it says, and who plays Clack's validator role in a two-party ceremony with no third expert?
3. Does the Lexon 0.3 grammar admit a canonical form (same meaning implies same AST), or does the canonical form problem block lossless graph deduplication in practice? Testable against the golden fixtures in sources/lexon/examples/.
4. Can the deontic meta-level ride as promise-typed overlay edges on Lexon's operational triples with zero grammar changes, or do the Promise and Commitment token primitives already constitute a partial deontic layer inside the language?
5. Where do felicity conditions sit in a taxonomy with no speech acts, and can Clack's proposed formalisation of implicature (p.32) coexist with deliberate outside-dyad indeterminacy, or are they mutually exclusive design goals?
