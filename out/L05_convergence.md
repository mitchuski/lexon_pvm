# L05: Merging traditional contracts and e-contracts (Idelberger) x 0xagentprivacy

Provenance: fetched from lexon.org/docs 2026-07-10, PDF canonical, no substitution; the working .txt has two-column interleaving that corrupts Listing 1 and mojibake in dashes and umlauts, so verify any code listing against the PDF before quoting in artefacts (Listing 3 sits in a clean column and was verified word-verbatim).

## What the letter claims (5 sentences maximum, no quotes over 14 words)

Idelberger implements one software-evaluation license contract in three stacks, Solidity, Prolog, and Lexon, and grades them on four criteria: meaning and spirit, syntax and semantics, functionality, and whether the artefact still reads as a contract to legal professionals (Sections 4, 5.3, 6). His central thesis is the anti-comprehension thesis: "the machine does not actually have to understand anything to be useful" (Abstract; restated in Section 7), provided translation is comprehensive and keeps syntax and semantics intact. He adopts Allen's contract-stack model, under which a contract is the whole heap of intent-signifying utterances, text, code, tweets, and marketing, with no fixed hierarchy and only case-by-case ranking (Section 3). His verdicts: Solidity delivers full functionality but zero legal legibility, Prolog captures the logic but depends fatally on term-naming discipline and has no native functionality, and Lexon passes all four criteria through direct AST mapping with no intermediary layer (Sections 6.1, 6.2, 6.3, 7). He judges Lexon the most promising and most advanced digital contract stack available, hedged by its keyword reliance, its dependence on the target normative environment, and the risk that it stagnates as an interesting experiment (Sections 6.3, 7, 8).

## Convergence findings (one subsection per assigned axis: A1, A5)

### A1: Performative agreement

**The anti-comprehension thesis relocates understanding, and so does the ceremony.** Idelberger moves understanding away from the machine and onto human readers: his top evaluation criterion is that the artefact can be read and understood by legal professionals and laypersons alike (Abstract; Section 5.3; the assessment following Listing 3 in Section 6.3). The Lexon compiler, in his account, understands nothing and does not have to. This confirms the structural half of the brief's candidate answer: like Understanding as Key, Idelberger makes the machine's job pure structure-preservation and places semantics at the human boundary. Both moves exclude the machine from meaning.

**But the direction is inverted.** Idelberger's relocation is receptive and public: anyone, especially counsel, can read the artefact. The ceremony's relocation is active and bilateral: exactly two parties prove understanding at formation via proverb derivation. Same relocation, opposite cardinality of understanders. This is the letter's sharpest A1 result, and it is a confirm-and-break: the brief's candidate answer holds in structure and fails in design goal.

**Formation is dissolved, not performed.** Under Allen's stack (Section 3), agreement is reconstructed case by case from the whole heap of utterances; the paper needs only the conclusion that the stack can, in principle, be a legal contract. There is no formation event anywhere in the model. The performative moment that Lexon (per L01 and L02) and the agreement ceremony both treat as constitutive is, for Idelberger, a judicial reconstruction after the fact. Genuine disagreement, recorded below under divergences.

**The arbiter is the felicity patch for automation.** The test case adds an ARBITER specifically because vague provisions, publication approval, a 24-hour removal window, breach, cannot be assessed automatically (Section 5 header block; Section 5.3). The ARBITER definition explicitly admits a natural person, a legal person, or a machine (Section 5.2, before Article 1). In the Lexon text the Arbiter holds the only two power clauses, Examine for Breach and Declare Breach (Listing 3, Section 6.3). Even the smallest realistic computable contract carries a designated third seat with bounded discretion, which connects forward to the A6 forum-key axis.

**Calibration for the synthesis (brief watch-for).** Idelberger's treatment of Lexon is sympathetic-critical, and he is a genuine third-party scholar (co-author of the Governatori et al. 2018 declarative-versus-imperative paper). Praise is comparative, not absolute: forefront of the field (Section 4), most promising candidate now (Section 8), most advanced stack available (Section 6.3), set against real criticism: keyword reliance named a main negative (Section 6.3), clause style demanding a change of thinking for non-programmers, dependence on the target normative environment, and an explicit stagnation risk (Sections 6.3, 7). This is the corpus's best independent voice: hosted on lexon.org, not captured by it.

### A5: Grammar to graph (keystone)

**Independent confirmation of the losslessness leg.** Idelberger identifies Lexon's defining characteristic as direct translation of natural language and legal prose to executable code via an abstract syntax tree, with no secondary intermediary layer, and credits exactly that property with preserving the structure, syntax, and higher-order meaning of the original document (Section 6.3 Technology; restated in Section 7). A critical third party independently names the AST, absent any intermediary, as the meaning-preserving carrier: precisely the property the run's Lexon-to-graph compiler target requires.

**Independent derivation of the missing-authoring-layer claim, stated negatively via the Prolog failure.** His Prolog verdict turns on term-naming discipline: much depends on the choice of terms and verbs, badly named facts can render the project unreadable or useless, and he calls for a system for setting facts, verbs, and rules, since without one inputting knowledge seems arbitrary (Sections 6.2, 7). That is the plan's A5 thesis inverted: raw triples without a controlled grammar are arbitrary symbol soup. Lexon's TERMS section is exactly such a system. Strong convergence; port to BONFIRES_NOTE as third-party evidence that free-form triple authoring fails.

**Retargetable AST.** He notes that Lexon's actionable words currently map to Solidity but could in principle map to other normative systems such as business rule systems (Section 6.3 Technology). If the AST retargets a rule engine, a triples or Cypher emitter is simply a third normative target. Direct support for HENNING_BRIEF probe (iii).

**Headline finding: the VRC slot is empty across the surveyed field.** His integrated contract stack layers out as: legal markup (LegalXML, Akoma Ntoso, LKIF; Section 2.1), logic languages plus ontologies and knowledge bases (Section 2.2), e-contract generations 1, 2, 2.5, and 3 per Wong (Section 2.3), metadata such as SPDX (Section 2.4), app-layer legal tech (Section 2.5), with the blockchain normative environment as enforcement (Section 6.1). Agentprivacy placement: MyTerms lands at markup and metadata, gates and Solidity at enforcement, Lexon at representation. The expected gap is confirmed, and it is double: no surveyed candidate for a formation layer, and no bilateral relationship memory layer. Every surveyed system is document- or contract-scoped; nothing persists the party-pair relationship across contracts.

**The gap is visible at grammar level inside Lexon itself.** The CONTRACTS per Licensee construct instantiates one executable contract per licensee, with actual amounts, assets, and identities per instance (Section 6.3 Implementation). Per-counterparty instantiation is per-edge contracting: each deployed instance is an edge from Licensor to one Licensee. But the memory is per-contract, not per-relationship: two contracts with the same licensee share nothing.

**Legal looks (brief read-for 3).** Verdicts: Solidity fails outright, unintelligible to legal professionals and laypersons alike (Section 6.1); Prolog fails with credit, expert-legible only, not contract-shaped (Sections 6.2, 7); Lexon passes, still reading and feeling like a contract to legal professionals (Section 6.3, after Listing 3). Consequence for MyTerms: of the surveyed field only Lexon clears the counsel-legibility gate, so a MyTerms term-set serialized in anything else predictably fails adoption with lawyers.

**KG lineage is already in his primer.** Section 2.2 defines ontologies, following Noy and McGuinness, as explicit format specifications of domain terms and their relations, with instantiated classes constituting a knowledge base. The letter already speaks graph; the run's triple-emission proposal extends his own Section 2.2 vocabulary to his Section 6.3 winner.

## Divergences and false friends

1. **Opposite cardinality of understanding (partial false friend with Understanding as Key).** Idelberger maximizes the audience of understanding: public legibility for judges and laypersons (Section 5.3). The RPP minimizes it: bilateral exclusivity, the proverb meaningless to everyone else. The slogan-level move is identical, machine excluded from semantics; the design goal is inverted.
2. **Readability is not demonstrated understanding.** None of his four criteria require any party to prove understanding at formation; an artefact that looks like a contract can be honoured unread. His framework cannot detect consent theatre, the exact failure mode the contractual privacy paradigm targets. Clean divergence worth preserving.
3. **Contract stack is a false friend for the agentprivacy layer stack.** Allen and Idelberger's stack is an interpretive evidence heap for courts, deciding which utterances count as the contract; the agentprivacy stack is an architecture of layered guarantees anchored beneath the layer they constrain (A8). They share the word stack and little else.
4. **Performative dilution.** For Idelberger no utterance is privileged as the binding act; for Lexon, a felicitous performative on an unstoppable substrate, and for the agreement ceremony, mutual promises binding the VRC, one discrete utterance-event is constitutive. Genuine disagreement between this letter and both projects.
5. **Four-corners maximalism (A5 pole).** He grades languages by how much meaning is computable and readable inside the document; the RPP vests decisive meaning in the bilateral decoder outside any document. He has no category for a deliberately opaque edge: an opaque proverb clause would score as a legibility defect in his criteria, where the RPP scores it as the point. This letter sits at the far public-legible pole of the plan's A5 divergence-to-preserve.
6. **False friend: the NLP-like approach phrasing (Abstract, Section 1).** Lexon is a deterministic grammar, not NLP; Idelberger's own Section 8 corrects the impression, noting Lexon does not rely on statistics-based AI. Do not let the abstract's phrasing import probabilistic-extraction connotations; the determinism is the run's entire point of contrast with LLM extraction.
7. **Contract-scoped versus relationship-scoped graphs.** His knowledge bases (Section 2.2) and contract instances (Section 6.3) are corpus fragments with no identity spine across documents. The promise-graph reading, one account carrying edges from many agreements, has no support in his model and must be marked as the run's extension, not his claim.
