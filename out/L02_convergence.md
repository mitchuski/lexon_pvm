# L02: Lexon - Legal Smart Contracts (Diedrich 2017) x 0xagentprivacy

*Provenance: PDF fetched from lexon.org/docs 2026-07-10, text extracted with pdftotext -layout; the working .txt has minor encoding artifacts (dashes, umlauts, quotes rendered as replacement characters), the PDF is canonical, no content substitution occurred.*

## What the letter claims (5 sentences maximum, no quotes over 14 words)

The 2017 whitepaper proposes a lawyer-readable language and virtual machine so lawyers, lawmakers, and judges can read and write legally correct smart contracts that draw on shared legal code libraries. It argues that reversibility must be provided by a system layer while the foundational blockchain layer stays irreversible, on the analogy of TCP adding guarantees over IP. The mechanism is a chain on top of a chain: an immutable information layer beneath a business-facts layer that a designated arbitration-key holder can reorganize (delete, reorder, add transactions, even amend code) within a gliding time window, with payouts held in implicit escrow until the window passes. It further claims multi-lingual and multi-jurisdictional reach: jurisdictions supply frameworks and dictionaries that contracts plug into, grammar itself is definable per language and jurisdiction, and laws would eventually be authored in the language under an Academy-style governance body. An appendix surveys Bitcoin Script, EVM opcodes, LLL, Serpent, Solidity, Corda/Kotlin, Rholang, and Hyperledger/Go to show that no existing smart-contract language is readable by the legal profession.

## Convergence findings (one subsection per assigned axis: A6, A7)

### A6. Accountable reversibility

The 2017 mechanics are fully specified in Section 1 (pp. 2-3) and Section 4.1.9 (pp. 16-17): an arbitration key, which is an ordinary published public key, reusable across unlimited contracts, multi-sig capable, and changeable by the arbitrators, plus a pre-set arbitration window (30 days in the example, settable to zero, which recovers today's finality), plus implicit escrow of payouts until the window passes. Within the window the arbitrator may retroactively change contract code and the event list. This is the design-space ancestor of the 2023 forum key.

The pattern the brief predicted, name the neutral, bound the window, keep the default final, is present verbatim in structure. Parties elect the arbitrator by including a published key, which the paper explicitly analogizes to a choice-of-law and choice-of-venue clause (Introduction, p. 3). The window is bounded and gliding (4.1.9, p. 17), and transactions older than the window are final and cannot be reversed. This converges with Accountable Wallet bounded disclosure and Privacy Pools bounded discretion.

Four further points of convergence:

1. **Zero-preparation neutral.** Arbitrators need no setup and need not even know they hold power until invoked; courts merely publish keys (Introduction, p. 2). This converges with Watcher Network designated observation without standing involvement: the neutral is passive until invoked.
2. **Reorg semantics without schism.** Reversal is a deterministic replay on the business-facts layer from the last non-reversed point, with retroactively inserted transactions; all hashes and consensus on the information layer remain valid, so no chain schism results, explicitly contrasted with the DAO hardfork (4.1.4-4.1.5, pp. 14-15; 3.4, p. 12). Legal fictions are recorded as post-event outcomes over an intact history; judges do not warp time, the chain captures legal history (2.5, p. 7).
3. **Trace retention.** The business-logic reorg must leave traces of deprecated previous truth, because prior records are indispensable to defend decisions made on the knowledge then available (4.1.2, pp. 13-14). This is the origin statement for what L01 files under A4: ceremony-record and evidence-substrate design.
4. **Finality as a consumer-protection variable.** Irreversibility protects merchants and criminals rather than consumers (3.4, p. 12), the same design instinct as the brief's claim that absolute finality is a bug for commerce. The DAO's 27-day freeze (4.1.3, p. 14) is cited as the empirical anchor: a bounded freeze window is what made recovery possible.

The paper's own compression of the mechanism is the cleanest bridge to accountable privacy: "Reversibility is functionally a temporary change in the burden of proof" (Section 4.1.9, p. 16). Discretion shifts who must prove what; it never edits the record.

The layering argument itself (4.1.1, p. 13), reversibility as a separate layer between blockchain and application with irreversibility retained as an enabling foundation, is formally A8 territory, but L02 carries the origin statement and it shares the anchoring instinct of the agentprivacy three-axis separation (also stated at 2.5, p. 7).

### A7. Terms as executable law

The 2017 paper answers the brief's read-for question directly: jurisdictional variation lives in libraries, not in the contract text. Three mechanisms carry it.

1. **Jurisdiction-as-framework** (5.1.1-5.1.2, p. 27). Regulations are expressible as OO-style frameworks, abstract frames with no processing code, brought to life by a concrete contract instantiation. The contract is the payload; the jurisdiction is the framework; different jurisdictions supply different frameworks and the same contract behaves differently under each.
2. **Definable grammar** (4.2.4, p. 26). The compiler runs two full cycles, the first defining grammar and new syntactic sugar, the second compiling the remaining source; 4.2.5 (p. 26) claims the principles work for German as well as English, with different language versions per jurisdiction. Section 5.1.2 elevates grammar definition to a candidate mechanism for expressing law itself.
3. **Governed common vocabulary** (5.2.1-5.2.2, pp. 28-29). Libraries of processable law as a common good, stewarded by an Academy modeled on the Academie francaise (a European Academy for Processible Law, credited to Harald Stieber, footnote 59). The MyTerms registry role held by Customer Commons occupies the same governance slot.

Beyond the mechanisms, the convergence with the contractual-privacy paradigm is direct. Joint composition (2.10, p. 9) has parties exchange machine-checkable drafts while an automated verifier answers how outcomes change under proposed edits: negotiation becomes computable counter-offers instead of take-it-or-leave-it consent theatre. Semantic meaningfulness checking (2.9, p. 9) means an unintelligible term fails verification rather than being deemed accepted, which is the operational replacement for fictional assent. The primacy-of-law stance (2.4, pp. 6-7; 4.1.10, p. 18) holds that law trumps crypto in business and that courts can create fictions the contract must absorb: terms that execute as written are also terms a court can read and amend as written, and comprehensibility to judges is framed as the best insurance against class actions. The paper also predicts a notary-like fact-oracle industry, with authoritatively established facts re-used like precedent and requiring upkeep (5.1.3, p. 28).

Two framing findings round out the axis. First, the dual-audience rhetoric: programming concepts are footnoted for lawyers (footnotes 4, 46-47, 50, 51, 52) while legal-economics arguments run in the body for programmers (2.1-2.4); the appendix shows raw code so lawyers experience unreadability directly (Section 6, p. 30); and 4.2.1 (pp. 21-23) renders the same token logic three ways, Lexon source, prose product, Solidity product. That triple rendering is the portable rhetorical move for a MyTerms whitepaper facing privacy engineers and policy people at once. Second, the cypherpunk grounding: Section 3.2.1 (p. 11) states the contractual-privacy motivation in Diedrich's own 2017 words, that metadata profiling opposes informational self-determination and that consent is given without protest because the business case is accepted. This is the consent-theatre diagnosis independently derived, a strong plural-recognition anchor for the MyTerms convergence.

## Divergences and false friends

1. **Trusted neutral versus trustless set membership (A6, the brief's predicted divergence, confirmed).** 2017 puts discretion in a key-holding human institution chosen by the parties; agentprivacy puts it in protocol-level association proofs. The 2017 text never considers removing the human from the loop; it celebrates the human, up to judges writing code (4.1.6, p. 15).
2. **Scope of discretion.** Arbitration by code (4.1.6, p. 15) lets the arbitrator retroactively add or change contract code and rewrite the event list: bounded rewriting of business history, not bounded disclosure. Accountable Wallet and Watcher discretion never touches state. The 2017 power is closer to a time-boxed backdoor with an audit trail than to a disclosure valve.
3. **False friend: escrow.** The 2017 escrow is an automatic suspension by the system layer, not a third-party escrow agent (Introduction, p. 2; 4.1.9, p. 16).
4. **False friend: reversibility.** It is re-derivation of business facts over an immutable log, never rollback of the chain; 4.1.5 explicitly rejects information-layer reorgs. Relatedly on A7, adjusting to jurisdiction is not surviving jurisdiction: jurisdiction-as-framework makes one contract behave differently per jurisdiction, while MyTerms wants the person's terms invariant with enforcement varying; footnote 17 (p. 6) notes Civil Law judges routinely strike clauses, so framework injection can override proffered terms.
5. **Direction of authorship (A7).** Lexon 2017 authors are lawyers, lawmakers, judges, and eventually governmental academies; the individual appears only as beneficiary of cheaper justice, never as the party proffering terms. MyTerms starts from the person proffering. The convergence is in the machinery, not the author. The Academy model (5.2.2, p. 29) is explicitly state-anchored and centralizing, in tension both with the paper's own cypherpunk grounding (3.2) and with a commons-governed MyTerms registry. The AI-judges ambition (5.1.1, p. 27, footnote 57) diverges from the deterministic-Swordsman versus probabilistic-Mage separation, and the paper itself concedes the fallibility of trained AI (5.1.3, p. 28), so that divergence is internal to the letter too.
6. **Internal tension: fractional reserve.** Section 4.1.12 (p. 20) extends the automatic escrow to allow uncovered transactions clawed back later, deliberately extending ripple effects into third-party accounts for unsecured tokens, in tension with the paper's own requirement to avoid uncontrolled ripple effects (2.5, p. 7) and with keep-the-default-final.
7. **Abandoned-branch watch (conjecture, pending L01 cross-check).** The 2023 forum key is described as a different approach to the same utility. Conjecture: event-list rewriting, arbitration-by-code, the deterministic-replay machinery, and the fractional-reserve extension did not survive to 2023; what shipped is the narrower reversible-transfer-by-designated-neutral. Likewise conjectured superseded: native Lexon opcodes with bi-directional deterministic decompilation (4.1.8, pp. 15-16), replaced by compiling to Solidity and other targets; the definable-grammar compiler-compiler and German grammar (4.2.4-4.2.5), given that shipped Lexon 0.3 per SOURCES.md is a fixed controlled grammar of 91 keywords in one English/Reyes subset; and the Academy and AI-judges ambitions, which have no visible 2023 successor. All of these are conjectures to verify against L01 and the site snapshots.

## Extractable mechanisms (what ports into agentprivacy V5/V6, concretely)

1. **Gliding-window automatic escrow.** Every payout sits in system escrow for a per-contract window (zero allowed), default-final after. Ports to the VRC lifecycle as a bounded contest window on ceremony effects (integrity-fraction adjustments, dissolution disputes) during which a pre-named neutral may act, with finality the default once the window glides past. CTR-LEX candidate for A6, forum-key versus association-set discretion, as the plan's P3 expects.
2. **Designation by key inclusion.** The neutral is designated by embedding a published public key: no onboarding, multi-sig capable, re-designatable, and possibly unaware until invoked. Ports directly to Watcher Network and Accountable Wallet neutral selection as a unilateral inclusion act in choice-of-venue style, rather than a bilateral enrollment ceremony.
3. **Two-layer record with retained previous truth.** An immutable signed event list below, re-derivable business facts above, deprecated states kept as traces. Ports to the epsilon(pi) evidence substrate: ceremony records as an append-only information layer over which relationship state is deterministically re-derivable, giving R < 1 bookkeeping plus defensible history, decisions provably made on the knowledge then available.
4. **Burden-of-proof vocabulary.** Reversibility modeled as a temporary shift in the burden of proof rather than a change of ownership (4.1.9, p. 16). Ports as V6 vocabulary for accountable privacy: a designated-discretion window shifts who must prove what and never edits history. One clean bridge sentence between forum keys and association proofs.
5. **Jurisdiction-as-framework compilation for MyTerms.** A person's term-set compiles as the payload article list against per-jurisdiction framework libraries supplying contract-law defaults. Concrete port: draft a MyTerms agreement as a Lexon article list, Payer/Payee style per the escrow golden fixture, with the Swordsman as a named role and the choice-of-venue slot filled by key inclusion (mechanism 2 reused). Feeds P3 artefact HENNING_BRIEF probe (i).
6. **Joint-composition verifier.** Automated semantic diff of proposed term edits, answering how outcomes change before signature. Ports to the agreement-ceremony negotiation phase: counter-offers to a proffered MyTerms set produce machine-readable outcome deltas, killing fictional assent operationally.
7. **Common-dictionary governance and fact oracles.** A stewarded registry of legal vocabulary as common good ports to MyTerms term-registry governance (the Customer Commons slot) and echoes the glossary-master census discipline on the PVM side. The fact-oracle pattern (5.1.3) ports to the epsilon(pi) substrate as attested, reusable facts; conjecture: the Swordsman is well shaped to be the person-side fact attestor.
8. **Triple-rendering rhetoric.** One source, three synchronized renderings (controlled text, prose, executable) with a small auditable compiler certifying sync (4.1.8, p. 15). Ports as the presentation pattern for a MyTerms whitepaper addressing engineers and policy people at once.

## Open questions (numbered, maximum 5)

1. Exactly which 2017 powers did the 2023 forum key drop: event-list rewriting, retroactive code amendment, or both? Needs a side-by-side with L01's forum-key section before the synthesis verdict on A6, and the same pass should settle whether the fractional-reserve clawback (4.1.12) was abandoned, marking the empirical boundary of how far bounded discretion can reach into third-party accounts.
2. Can the arbitration window be expressed as a promise in the Promise Theory sense: both parties voluntarily promise a bounded discretion to the named neutral, making the system-layer escrow the kept promise rather than an imposition?
3. Deterministic replay requires the full signed event list; who may read it during arbitration, and does invoking the neutral leak the relationship graph? The 2017 mechanism is silent on log confidentiality, so this is the A4 privacy question applied to it. A related hybrid to explore: a neutral defined as a set-membership proof over published court keys, association-set trustlessness with forum-key accountability.
4. Did the definable-grammar two-cycle compiler survive in any form to Lexon 0.3, or is the fixed 91-keyword grammar the whole story? This determines whether MyTerms per-jurisdiction compilation can use grammar variants or only vocabulary and libraries, and whether the claimed German and English grammars (4.2.5) would produce identical subject-predicate-object triples, which the A5 keystone needs to survive translation.
5. Can one MyTerms term-set remain semantically invariant while compiled against divergent jurisdiction frameworks, given the clause-striking reality of footnote 17: what is the invariance proof obligation, who may author person-side frameworks with standing equal to state actors, and can the framework direction be inverted so the person's proffered terms are the framework a counterparty's contract must instantiate?
