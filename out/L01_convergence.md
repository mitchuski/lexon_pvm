# L01: The Holy Grail of Computational Law (Diedrich 2023) x 0xagentprivacy

*Provenance: the brief marked L01 as in hand, but no PDF was present; the source was fetched 2026-07-10 from the lexon.org/docs manifest URL per the ANALYSIS_PLAN failure rule, and locators were verified against a pdftotext -layout extraction with known mojibake and two-column artifacts near Source 5 and Figure 10.*

## What the letter claims

Lexon compiles controlled English, a subject-predicate-object grammar parsed by GLR into higher-order logic, into Javascript or Solidity, presenting itself as the realization of the Leibniz-to-Genesereth computational-law program. On an unstoppable blockchain, contract prose becomes felicitous performative speech in Austin's sense (fn. 7, credited to David Bovil in the letter's spelling), with an artificial judge built into every deployed contract. Meaning does not live in nouns, which are neutral markers; it travels in the relations between them, and the one word Lexon actually understands is transfer. The Programmable Token anchors obligations below the contract layer: gates are voluntary per-account restrictions, joined by Commitment, Promise, Cheque, Escrow, Reversibility, and DAO primitives that dissolve star-shaped smart contracts into a peer-to-peer web. Off-chain, a hash-chained signed log (the micro blockchain) gives trustless private contracting with fully private state, and the Robotic Laws appendix projects Lexon as the language lawmakers will use to embed limitations verbatim into autonomous machines.

## Convergence findings

### A1. Performative agreement

The letter's central move is that deployment on an unstoppable substrate makes contract prose felicitous: "these words become true by uttering them" (Introduction, pg. 1-2, fn. 7 passage). The footnote cites Austin's 1955 How to Do Things with Words and credits the observation to David Bovil (the letter's spelling; ANALYSIS_PLAN spells Bovill). The agreement ceremony in the RPP makes the same performative claim from the identity side, so the convergence is real at the level of speech-act framing.

Three structural details deepen it. First, fn. 3 (pg. 1) states that the document's meaning is reflected in the abstract syntax trees the compiler creates: the utterance is felicitous because the parse is the meaning. Second, felicity is substrate-borne rather than counterparty-borne: the letter says illocution needs neither judges nor litigators, because an artificial judge is built into every digital contract (Introduction, pg. 2), and after deployment no further assent is needed. Third, role binding is itself a performative naming act (Call Parameters, pg. 6-7): if a role was not defined earlier, the first call assigns it, and thereafter only the same person can perform the role or the call is rejected. The letter compares this to value binding in functional languages.

Two passing items carried forward per the brief. The appendix AST (Figure 10, pg. 29) decomposes the escrow recital into one subject fanning into four typed predicates, that is four triples: (Payer, pays-into-escrow, Amount), (Payer, appoints, Payee), (Payer, appoints, Arbiter), (Payer, fixes, Fee). This is the requested evidence that a Lexon text is a lossless triple serialisation, held for the Bonfires note. And the single-verb claim (LANGUAGE, pg. 3): everything in Lexon reduces to transfer plus qualifiers, a direct structural parallel to the lattice edge as the single primitive of sovereignty transition.

The Wittgenstein grammar claim (LANGUAGE, pg. 2, fn. 16, connection first noted by Andrea Leiter) says the grammar overlays natural language and higher-order logic in the way Wittgenstein demanded; it feeds the A5 keystone handled in synthesis.

### A2. Gates as promises

The gate definition (EXTENSIBILITY, pg. 14) is a voluntary, per-account restriction on the use of the account's tokens, usable to express obligations, which smart contracts generally cannot. Fn. 75 adds that gates let individual owners accept voluntary restrictions, called the very nature of contracts, for one account only, at any time. This is Promise Theory in different vocabulary: only the autonomous agent binds itself, and the binding is local to the promiser. The letter even uses the word promise for what a gate enables, saying the power of gates lies in the unbreakable promise they permit (pg. 14).

The gate lifecycle (pg. 14-15, Table 3 pg. 34) maps cleanly to promise polarity: gate_set and gate_prepare are the owner's (+) promise about its own behavior; gate_activate by an optional keeper who confirms the settings is the (-) acceptance promise; gate_close under pre-declared rules is revocation. An account can only be gated with the consent of both owner and keeper when a keeper exists (pg. 15), which is the one genuinely bilateral moment in the system.

The token primitives are literally named Commitment and Promise (INTERACTION, pg. 12-13; signatures pg. 33). Commitment: tokens blocked for a named receiver and time, releasable by the receiver, can time out, one per account. Promise: a weaker concept, no tokens blocked, uncovered, made for a specific amount to a specific receiver who can forgive it, unchangeable by the giver, non-expiring, reduced by any transfer to the receiver, multiple allowed. Signatures: make(promissee, amount), forgive(promissor). The Gate Interface exposes 24 on* event hooks (Table 4, pg. 34), including onSending, onReceiving, onCommitting, onMaking, onForgiving, onAcceding, onReversing: a complete named enforcement surface for boundary promises. Gates also achieve promise semantics without escrowed hostage value: Figures 3 and 4 (pg. 14) show the gate eliminating the pay-in lock-up step of the smart-contract pattern.

### A3. Topology of trust and promise graphs

The letter makes the topology argument empirically: the relationship structure of a complex smart contract typically has a star shape, revealing its centralizing effect (Decentralization of Logic, pg. 15, Figure 5), and fn. 80 grounds this in the author's graphical Ethereum transaction browser at IBM, where these shapes emerged as the prevalent patterns. Per-account gates replace the star with a web of direct peer-to-peer connections (Figure 6), described as more robust and better suited for growth because it allows incremental development of relationships. This is the bilateral-VRC argument against issuer-centric credentials, made from the token side.

Two further properties matter for promise graphs. Authority location: building blocks are malleable with authority to change accruing to exactly those parties who would stand to lose from a change (pg. 15), which is edge governance by edge stakeholders. Composition without global standards: gates in a transaction are independent and oblivious of each other, forming a loosely coupled emergent system (Dynamic Stacking of Logic, Figure 7, pg. 15-16), and fn. 81 records an engineered spill-over containment: a trustless custody mechanism prevents a deeper gate from vetoing a transfer for everyone involved. Cross-contract promise density is native: one account simultaneously carries approvals, one commitment, multiple promises, a gate, and reversibility grants (pg. 12-13), so the same account is a node in many agreements at once, which is exactly the promise-graph-spanning-contract-boundaries claim of the run's thesis.

Conjecture, recorded per the brief and marked as conjecture: gate-webs raise the effective scaling exponent k in the stratum-weighted network term (1 + Σ wᵢnᵢ/N₀)^k relative to star topology, because each account contributes independent promise-making capacity (O(N) distributed) where a star routes through one hub's fixed logic (O(1)). Falsification condition: simulate both topologies with equal promise counts and show equal value growth.

### A4. Private evidence chains

The micro blockchain (History and The Micro Blockchain, pg. 8) is a hash-chained signed log held only by the parties. Entry format (Figure 2): hash, timestamp, role, clause or noun, signature, where the hash is SHA-256 over the entire log file up to that point and the signature covers everything before it in the entry, yielding an unforgeable trace of who did what, when. The privacy claim is explicit: contract state remains perfectly private, at administrative cost magnitudes lower than a chain, by the same reiterative hash-and-sign method (fn. 55 cites Nakamoto 2008). This is the reconstruction ceiling R < 1 implemented as bookkeeping: full evidence for the parties, no state reconstruction for outsiders.

The letter is honest about the trade: someone could lose the log, there is no consensus mechanism when two parties act at nearly the same time, and logs do not support coins (pg. 8). Execution is turn-based and bilateral: the whole contract system (state, code, log, instructions) persists, bundles into contract.tgz, and travels to the counterparty who performs the next step (Persistence, Bundling, Email, pg. 8-9), with fn. 56 stating that a state is confirmed by its log. A forthcoming microclient (pg. 9) would handle the log exchange peer-to-peer, which converges on exactly the bilateral evidence-exchange channel a VRC needs. The appendix (pg. 23-24, 28) shows the working implementation: RSA-SHA256 role signatures, whole-file SHA-256 per entry, and a truncated 12-hex-character hash prefix carried into the next entry.

### A8. Anchoring below the agent

The anchoring claim appears twice: interventions like partial diversion or reversal must be anchored at the token-implementation level, not in contract logic (Introduction, pg. 2, fn. 12), and what an underlying system does not provide, a language cannot support (Utility, pg. 11). This is identical in form to the three-axis separation claim: guarantees must anchor beneath the layer they constrain. Fn. 84 supplies the database-locking analogy: application-level integrity is impossible without atomic primitives in the storage layer. The letter names a boundary concept, the trustless membrane (Utility, pg. 11): the region of functionality performable in one coherently protected sequence without trusting a third party for an intermediate step, and the token's job is to extend its scope. Proofing Programmable Money (pg. 16, appendix pg. 30-31) applies this to documented power abuse, withheld payments punishing the productive party, and again locates the fix in the token layer.

The Robotic Laws appendix (pg. 31) and Conclusion (pg. 18) extend the anchoring downward into machines: laws written in Lexon, authored by elected lawmakers, implemented verbatim in autonomous machines, with Asimov cited as direction of thought and his self-contradictions acknowledged. On determinism, Lexon self-positions as the symbolic complement to generative AI, adding agency, transparency, and reliability (fn. 90, pg. 18), with ML relegated to the preparatory writing phase: structurally the Swordsman/Mage split with Lexon on the Swordsman side. The Reversibility forum-key mechanism (pg. 13, signatures pg. 33), a designated forum such as a court or notary with bounded, owner-granted, time-windowed discretion to reverse, is noted here only because it is implemented at the token layer; full A6 treatment belongs to the L02 chronicle.

## Divergences and false friends

**Unilateral felicity (A1).** Lexon felicity is unilateral after deploy: the Payer instantiates and appoints Payee and Arbiter in one constructor call, with no bilateral co-signature, cost signal, proverb derivation, or personhood grounding. RPP ceremony felicity requires two First Persons uttering mutual promises. Conjecture, marked: a Lexon instantiation is at best a degenerate one-sided agreement ceremony. In trustful Javascript mode (fn. 45, pg. 7 concedes anyone could manipulate anything), the performative force collapses and only the signed log survives as evidence, so the word performative names two different felicity conditions: substrate-conditional in Lexon, counterparty-conditional in the RPP. Terminological convergence risk.

**Promise is a false friend (A2).** Lexon's Promise is the weakest primitive in its ladder, weaker than Commitment: uncovered, unblocked, non-expiring, forgivable only by the receiver. The VRC promise bundle is the strongest binding in agentprivacy: bilateral, co-signed, mutually assessed. Same word, opposite position in the strength ordering. Lexon promises and commitments are strictly unilateral; the receiver's only verbs are forgive, release, deposit. The keeper is also a third-party power channel, able to update parameters and close the gate, so a gated account's obligation is co-administered, unlike a Swordsman boundary promise held by the sovereign alone. And gates constrain token use only: the convergence is exact at the bookkeeping layer and analogical above it.

**The web has a hidden hub (A3).** The gate web is logically decentralized but infrastructurally mediated: every gate, promise, and commitment lives inside one deployed token contract (Table 3 is a single contract's ABI). The star is removed at the relationship layer and reintroduced one stratum down. The honest mapping of Figure 6 is topology of authority, not topology of data. Lexon's web is also transactional and ad hoc, edges materializing per transfer, where the VRC graph is a persistent relationship graph.

**Evidence without identity or time (A4).** Timestamps are self-declared plain text with no external anchor, and roles bind to any name, alias, number, or key, so the log proves key-continuity of a role and internal sequence, not personhood or wall-clock truth. VRC redundancy (both parties hold full copies) mitigates log loss but does not supply the missing consensus for near-simultaneous acts; that gap needs co-signing discipline, not copies. The email transport (Source 9, pg. 9) leaks relationship metadata to mail providers: content privacy without graph privacy.

**Who writes the boundary (A8).** The Robotic Laws are state-authored, universal, democratically approved; the Swordsman charter is sovereign-authored and per-person. The letter is unambiguous about its side. This is a genuine divergence to preserve, not a bug: Lexon anchors public law below private agents; agentprivacy anchors private boundaries below public exposure, with the same mechanism aimed by different hands. Two smaller flags: the trustless membrane is execution-scoped atomicity, mapping most cleanly onto Φ_agent alone (conjecture, marked), where the three-axis separation is a standing multiplicative property; and unbiased in the letter is a determinism claim, not a values claim, since bias moves upstream into whoever writes the Lexon text.

## Extractable mechanisms

1. **First-use role binding** (A1): the bind-once-then-verify rule (first call assigns the role, subsequent calls must match or reject) as the name-binding model inside V5 agreement ceremony records.
2. **The felicity split** (A1): distinguish execution felicity (substrate unstoppability) from relational felicity (bilateral assessment) as two registers in the PVM; a VRC can carry both, with the micro-blockchain log providing the execution half.
3. **AST as co-signed meaning object** (A1): if agreement text is authored in controlled grammar, the parse tree is the shared meaning artifact both parties co-sign, closing the gap between what was said and what was agreed.
4. **Graded promise-strength ladder** (A2): Approval, Commitment, Promise as three canonical strength classes for VRC promise typing; all three are (+) promises differing in enforcement backing.
5. **Gate lifecycle as boundary-promise lifecycle** (A2): set and prepare, activate with bilateral consent, update parameters, close; the activation consent is the minimal co-signature already native to Lexon and the natural splice point for full VRC co-signing.
6. **The 24 on* hooks mapped to polarity** (A2): onSending and onReceiving as (-) assessment points, onMaking and onCommitting as (+) emission events, onForgiving and onReleasing as dissolution, onReversing and onAcceding as accountability. Feeds HENNING_BRIEF probe (ii) directly. Also forgive(promissor) as a receiver-side unilateral release primitive for VRC release ceremonies.
7. **Stratum as promise popcount** (A3): define an account's stratum as the count of active promise-class objects on it (approvals, commitment, promises, gates, reversibility grants); Table 3 gives an enumerable basis. Candidate CTR-LEX seed, together with the gate-web exponent conjecture above.
8. **Cascade containment rule** (A3): fn. 81's no-deep-veto property as a portable boundedness condition on promise-graph cascade terms in V6, plus the design test that only edge endpoints may mutate an edge.
9. **The 5-field log entry as ceremony-record serialization** (A4): role as participant, clause or fixed noun as promise event type (state changes logged in the contract's own vocabulary, keeping evidence human-readable), signature as attestation, hash chain as integrity fraction. Candidate ε(π) evidence substrate and CTR-LEX seed. With it: bundle-and-send (contract.tgz turn-passing) as the minimal zero-infrastructure ceremony transport, and fn. 56's one-line state semantics, relationship state derivable from and only from the record chain. Conjecture, marked: the micro blockchain realizes R < 1 structurally because the evidence chain never leaves the parties; falsifiable if a third party can reconstruct state from transport metadata alone.
10. **The anchoring evidence line** (A8): the Utility pg. 11 principle, obligations enforced at layer n require primitives at layer n-1, as the L01 citation for the V6 anchoring claim-cluster. Falsification condition: exhibit a contract-layer-only mechanism achieving reversal or diversion with equal guarantees. Corollaries: Swordsman boundary promises should compile to substrate-level hooks rather than agent-level policy; the trustless membrane becomes a nameable, measurable V5 perimeter concept; and stacked gates permit the coexistence test of a state-authored Robotic-Law gate and a sovereign charter gate on one account, a concrete joint-work probe with Diedrich.

## Open questions

1. Is a Lexon contract instantiation an agreement ceremony in the RPP sense? Conjecture: yes but degenerate and unilateral; bilateral proverb, cost signal, and personhood grounding could be added as gates or co-signature requirements without changing the grammar, and the Multi-Signature account feature (pg. 12) suggests the substrate already permits a bilateral constructor.
2. What is the keeper's promise polarity: a (-) acceptance-promise to administer, or a standing imposition the owner pre-accepted? The answer decides whether a gated account is autonomous in the Bergstra/Burgess sense, and whether gate re-use across unlimited accounts (pg. 15) makes gates the VRC-template mechanism.
3. Does gate-web topology change the scaling exponent k or only the multiplier inside the stratum sum, and is the single shared token contract a hidden hub that the PVM's per-stratum weights already price in?
4. Can mutual log copies plus cross-signing on every exchange substitute for consensus on near-simultaneous entries, and does the truncated 48-bit hash prefix (appendix pg. 24) leave a collision gap to flag for Diedrich? Related: does emailing contract.tgz defeat the perfectly-private claim at the graph layer, with the P2P microclient as the intended fix?
5. Is the claim that obligations require account-level anchoring (gates express what smart contracts generally cannot) provable, or an efficiency claim? V6 discipline requires classing it as proven versus conjectured before citing, and the same test applies to whether Lexon's determinism survives oracle-fed gate triggers (pg. 14).
